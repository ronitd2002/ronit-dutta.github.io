Trapped Ion Quantum Simulation

Visiting Researcher at Pagano Lab, Rice University | Houston, USA | Oct 2023 - July 2024

Currently working on two projects parallelly while more lined up for further. We use Yb171+ ions for quantum simulation setup which is Doppler cooled by 369.5nm transition. In previous experiments, it has been observed that far more optimal cooling times and bandwidth can be achieved with higher power low noise Doppler beams. But buying such lasers in the power range of 30-70mW is expensive, and this is where injection-locking comes in. This commonly used technique in AMO-labs uses a low power but low phase and amplitude noise Master Laser , a 369.5nm Moglabs ECLDL in our case, to lock the frequency and phase of high power but also noisy and broader band Slave Laser, a 372nm Nichia's laser diode.

This is done by controlling the current and temperature of the slave laser diode. In this project, I designed a compact module packing the diode and Thermoelectric coolers diffused with Nitrogen to avoid dew. A PID-based analog circuit to control the TECs is designed to keep the temperature stable while the current is controlled by Thorlabs LDC202C. To adjust for the ambient temperature drifts in the lab, injection lock efficiency is digitally processed from a Fabry-Perot cavity's spectra and fed back to the current controller to change the setpoint accordingly.

The present setup also required a new placement of ovens for Yb and Ba atoms. For this, we designed a new and mechanically simpler oven, placing it closer to the trap while maximising the Joule heating and collimation while minimising conductive heat loss. To test the oven I also designed an adapter/testing mount for our spare chamber. Some parts are currently being machined offshore, while the rest in-house.

Negative Differential Thermal Conductance by Photonic Heat Transport

Internship project at Quantum Research Center, TII | Abu Dhabi, UAE | Aug 2021 - Feb 2022

The negative differential thermal conductance (NDTC), basically increasing thermal bias giving decreasing heat flux, provides a key mechanism for realizing thermal transistors and amplifiers. This exotic effect has been the object of an extensive theoretical investigation, but the implementation is still limited to a few specific physical systems. In this project supervised by Prof. Luigi Amico, we devise a generalized mechanism to engineer NDTC in any electronic circuit. We show how photonic heat transport between elements of an electronic circuit can show NDTC if one of the elements undergoes a phase transition in conductivity. And to demonstrate this, we model photon-mediated transport in a superconducting circuit around the critical temperature of a thin film metal island. The calculation is self-consistent and includes subtler effects of electron-phonon coupling as well. Our paper was accepted by Physical Review B

Turbulence in Exciton Polariton Condensates

Charpak Lab Scholar | PhLAM, CNRS | Lille, France | May 2022 - July 2022

Exciton-Polaritons are hybrid light-matter quasiparticles, eigenstates of strongly coupled exciton(bound electron-hole pair) and a photon(trapped in a Bragg microcavity). As Jaynes-Cummings Hamiltonian would say, for every exciton-photon pair, there would be 2 polaritonic modes, provided this pair has the same momentum in-plane to the microcavity. A Fourier space spectroscopy image on the right shows these modes for every momenta. These are now interacting Bosonic particles trapped in 2D. The project aimed to perform a picosecond resolution study of superfluid(like an interacting BEC) exciton-polariton condensate flowing against a defect-acting as an obstacle to observing the shredding of quantized vortices. These vortices interact and cluster together creating an inverse cascade of energy, a signature of 2D turbulence. Under the guidance of CNRS Researcher Alberto Amo I got the opportunity to build up my first experiment, starting from optical alignments, electronics, setting up the optomechanics and cryogenics, characterizing the dispersion curves and condensation thresholds using Fourier space spectroscopy to finally probe the desired phenomena. The 2-month internship was funded by the Charpak Lab Scholarship awarded to 30 students by the French Embassy in India.

Master's Thesis Project

Study of nonequilibrium physics in steady state exciton-polariton condensates

Endowment Prize for Best Masters Thesis | Advisor: Prof. Vishwanath Shukla | Aug 2022 - March 2023

Having developed a robust code to simulate coherent and incoherent condensation of exciton-polaritons in a microcavity, we have headed on to theoretically model nonequilibrium physics like turbulence and KPZ-diffusion in the condensate. In the right, you see our simulation of an incoherent condensation of polaritons using a Gaussian pump with added noise in its intensity. Due to the collision of radially outwards waves and the reflected waves quantized vortices are excited, which at a threshold of nonlinearity in the system, interact to form larger clusters of vortices, thereby transferring incompressible kinetic energy from smaller to larger length scales. This is seen as Kolmogorov's -5/3 power law dependency in the spectrum of incompressible kinetic energy. Other details on the theory, numerical scheme and analysis can be found in the Thesis.
